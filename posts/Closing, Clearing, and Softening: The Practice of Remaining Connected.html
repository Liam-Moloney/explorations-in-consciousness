Closing, Clearing, and Softening: The Practice of Remaining Connected

16 March 2026 — 18:15

I should probably begin by saying that I am not, in the conventional sense, a meditator. I do not sit down with the deliberate intention of meditating in the way many people do. Yet there is something I practice that belongs to the same family of inner disciplines. The practice is simple and functional: I close and clear my aura.

Closing and clearing the aura, as I understand it, is not a decorative idea but a practical one. When the aura is closed and clear, three conditions naturally appear together: wholeness, connection, and unity. When any of those conditions weaken, it is usually because the aura is no longer closed or no longer clear. So the discipline becomes very straightforward. If I notice that I am not whole, not connected, or not unified, I return to the same action: close the aura, clear the aura, and remain with the process until connection is restored.

Watching people over time, it becomes apparent that meditation itself exists in several different relationships with human behaviour. Some people go through life without meditating at all. A number of them have never even heard of meditation, and others see no reason to take it seriously. Another group has heard about meditation and tried it occasionally, but their relationship with it is loose. They are unsure whether they “get there” or not. Then there are people who take meditation more seriously. They might practice once or twice a week, sometimes more. Some enjoy it. Others approach it as a kind of moral or spiritual hygiene. But across these categories something interesting often remains true: meditation is something people do, yet it does not always fundamentally change the way they behave.

Then occasionally you meet someone who approaches the practice differently. In my case that person was Connie Huebner. Connie does healing work and facilitates meditation, but what is distinctive about her approach is that she coaches herself through meditation. She is not sitting there trying to empty the mind. Her attention rests in the heart and in an awareness of the Divine Mother. The result is that meditation becomes active and relational rather than passive.

Connie enjoys meditating. She enjoys healing. I discovered something slightly different about myself. I do not especially enjoy the process of healing in the same way she does. What I prefer is the state of having been healed.

One of the practical things Connie taught me was the discipline of closing and clearing the aura. When I began experimenting with it, I noticed a very consistent pattern. If my aura was closed and clear, I was connected. The connection appeared reliably. The opposite also proved true. When I felt disconnected, it was usually safe to assume that the aura was open, unclear, or both.

That observation gradually made me attentive to connection itself. I began to watch for it. Over time the practice became easier. If I noticed disconnection, I knew what to do: close the aura, clear the aura, and re-establish connection.

At some point another realization appeared. No one else can do this work for you. There is no automatic mechanism that sweeps the mind clean. Certain medications may influence mood or perception, but the basic responsibility for the state of your own mind remains yours. You are sovereign over it.

That sovereignty carries a practical implication. When disconnection appears, the first response is not to analyse the world or blame circumstances. The first response is simply to close and clear the aura and restore connection. With enough repetition the system becomes familiar with the movement. By mid-March 2026 I find that I am rarely disconnected for long, and I am extremely aware of whether connection is present or not.

Alongside this discipline Connie uses another tool during meditation and healing sessions. It is a small instruction that is rarely explained in technical terms. The instruction is simply this: soften.

During meditation someone might be told to soften in the crown or soften in the heart. There is usually no elaborate explanation attached to the instruction. Instead we turn our attention toward the area in question and intend softness. Somehow the system understands the gesture. The softening happens.

In practice this instruction often appears when something uncomfortable arises during meditation. A sensation appears, or a thought, or an emotional movement. Perhaps the mind recoils slightly. Rather than analysing the experience, the instruction is simply repeated: soften.

Over time it becomes clear that the command is not limited to meditation. It is a general way of responding to difficulty. If the aura is closed and clear, connection remains present. When discomfort appears—whether in meditation, in conversation, or in solitude—the same instruction applies. Soften.

The interesting thing about softening is that it restores flow. It is deliberately unspecific. It does not attempt to solve the difficulty intellectually. Instead it loosens the mind’s grip on whatever interpretation has begun to crystallize. The system releases its tension, and the movement of awareness resumes.

In that sense the practices learned in meditation translate directly into ordinary life. We close the aura. We clear the aura. Connection appears. And when disturbances arise—as they inevitably will—the response is simple.

Soften.

And if there is any meaningful measure of a person’s development, it probably does not lie in how well they meditate. The real measure is something far more ordinary and far more difficult.

It is the ability to be kind to other people.
