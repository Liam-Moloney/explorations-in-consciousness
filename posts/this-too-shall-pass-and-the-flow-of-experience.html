This Too Shall Pass and the Flow of Experience

16 March 2026 — 15:17

There is a way of working with the language people already use in ordinary life that can quietly shift its meaning without replacing it. Instead of introducing entirely new terminology, the existing phrases can be seen more clearly. When that happens, something that once sounded like consolation begins to function as realization.

Take the phrase often attributed to Jesus: “This too shall pass.”

In everyday life the sentence is usually understood as reassurance. A person who is suffering is reminded that the difficulty will not last forever. Time will move forward, circumstances will change, and eventually the experience will fade. The phrase comforts the mind by placing relief somewhere in the future.

But the statement can also be heard differently.

If something shall pass, then passing is not merely a future event. Passing is the nature of the moment itself. Time does not sit still waiting to carry the experience away. The experience is already moving within time. It is already passing.

When this is seen clearly, the phrase stops being simple consolation and becomes a small revelation about the structure of experience.

Most people do not notice the underlying flow of awareness. They do not know that much of the time they are already moving within it. Nor do they recognize when the mind has tightened and stepped out of that continuity. They simply feel bad when contraction appears. Something seems wrong, and they assume the feeling must be endured until circumstances change.

But if the moment is already passing, then the solidity of the experience begins to dissolve slightly. What seemed fixed is revealed to be moving. The disturbance is not a permanent object sitting in the mind; it is a configuration of attention that is being continuously renewed.

In that sense the phrase points toward a deeper possibility.

The only thing that keeps the moment psychologically present is the mind’s continued attention to it. Attention holds the interpretation in place. When attention relaxes, the moment resumes its natural movement through time.

This is where the practice becomes visible.

When contraction appears, the response is not argument or analysis. The response is simply to soften. Softening releases the grip around the interpretation that the mind has produced. As the contraction relaxes, awareness returns to the continuity that was already there.

Flow reappears because it had never actually disappeared. The system simply stopped resisting the movement that was already underway.

Seen this way, the phrase “this too shall pass” contains more than reassurance. It quietly describes a property of time itself. Everything that appears in experience is already passing.

And when the mind remembers that, the instruction becomes clear.

Soften.

Allow attention to release its grip on the moment and return to the flow of radiance that was never truly interrupted.
