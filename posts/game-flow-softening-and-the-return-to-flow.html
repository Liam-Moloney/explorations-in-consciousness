Game Flow, Softening, and the Return to Flow

15 March 2026 — Evening Reflection

Let me ground this idea in something practical.

I play a word game. It’s an anagram game with thirty-second rounds. You receive nine letters and have thirty seconds to make the longest word you can.

When I am in form, something interesting happens. I don’t merely find the longest word — I know it is the longest word.

If I make a nine-letter word, that certainty is obvious. But the more interesting case is when I find, say, a seven-letter word and I know instinctively that there is no eight and no nine. I am correct more than ninety-five percent of the time when I’m in that state. The mind is operating with clarity and speed, but it is also operating with trust. There is a feeling of completion. The answer lands and the system knows it has landed.

That is game flow.

But sometimes I sit down to play and that flow is not there. The letters appear, the clock runs, and the mind feels slightly slower, slightly heavier, slightly unsure. And this is where something new has begun to appear for me.

The old response is familiar. The old response is to interpret the situation: “I’m not in form today.” Or “It’s just one of those rounds.” Or perhaps to push harder, to strain mentally in the hope of forcing the flow to return.

But I am beginning to see that a different option exists.

Instead of obsessing over the absence of flow, I can soften into the desire for flow.

This is a subtle shift. It means that when the difficulty appears — when the mind feels blocked or uncertain — that difficulty itself becomes the signal to soften.

Previously we were often obstructed by our own beliefs about performance. If the mind said we were not in form, we accepted the verdict and continued from that position. The belief itself became the obstruction.

But if consciousness is placed at the centre of attention, something else becomes possible.

When the flow is absent, the absence itself becomes information.

It is the moment to soften.

Softening does not mean abandoning the game or pretending the difficulty is not there. It means relaxing the interpretive tension around it. The letters are still there. The clock is still running. But the mind releases its grip on the story about why the flow is missing.

Then something interesting happens.

The difficulty begins to function like a signpost.

Instead of stopping the process, it becomes the place where the process adjusts itself. The moment of obstruction becomes the moment of recalibration. The mind relaxes its contraction, and the system has another opportunity to align with flow.

This is what I mean when I talk about closing and clearing the aura.

A closed aura is simply a mind that is not leaking energy into unnecessary interpretation. A clear aura is a mind that is not clouded by accumulated narratives about success and failure. When the system is both closed and clear, intention becomes clean. You can move forward without the mind fighting itself.

And then the difficulties are no longer barriers.

They become instructions.

Each moment of contraction points to where softening is needed. Each softening reopens the possibility of flow. And once this pattern becomes familiar, the mind stops fearing difficulty because difficulty itself becomes part of the navigation system.

The goal is no longer to avoid interruption.

The goal is to recognise interruption quickly enough that softening can restore movement.

In that sense this practice is not about standing still inside a peaceful state. It is about learning how to move through resistance without becoming trapped by it.

The game simply makes the process visible. But the principle is much larger than the game.

Flow is not guaranteed.

But the return to flow can be learned.
