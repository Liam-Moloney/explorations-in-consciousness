Softening and the Minister of the Mind

15 March 2026 — Late Reflection

There is another aspect of this practice that needs to be recorded because it is extremely subtle. What we are talking about in meditation, in exploration, or in quiet observation is not something dramatic. It does not arrive with obvious signals. It begins very small.

At first it exists only as a faint mental reflex.

Most of the time we live inside automatic reactions. A contraction appears in the mind — frustration, hesitation, doubt, resistance — and almost instantly we interpret it. We conclude something about ourselves: that we are failing, that we are diminished, that something has gone wrong. These interpretations happen so quickly that they feel natural. They feel like the truth.

But the practice we are exploring interrupts that sequence.

Instead of treating contraction as evidence of failure, contraction becomes a signal. It becomes information. And the response to that signal is the command to soften.

At the beginning this is not automatic. It may take many hours of meditation or quiet observation to nurture this tiny reflex. The mind has been trained for years to react in the opposite direction — to tighten around difficulty rather than relax into it.

So the first stage of the practice is simply recognition. You notice the contraction. You notice the moment when the mind begins to form the familiar interpretation. That is the moment where a new response becomes possible.

You soften.

This command is deliberately non-specific. In fact it works precisely because it is non-specific. It functions in much the same way as the instruction to “let go.” When someone says “let go,” you may not even be aware that you are holding anything, yet the mind understands the gesture. Something releases. Something loosens.

The command to soften operates at the same level. It is not a technical instruction and it does not require the mind to understand exactly what it is doing. The instruction is simply given: soften.

At that moment another idea becomes important: you are the minister for your own mind.

This is a sovereign position. No one else occupies it. No one else can perform it for you. The responsibility for the state of your headspace belongs to you alone. That does not mean controlling every thought or emotion; it means choosing how the mind responds when contraction appears.

Most of the time the reflex will not appear automatically. The old habits are too strong. That is why the practice must be brought out of the darkness. It must be named and recognised. Once you see it clearly, it becomes easier to notice the moments when it is needed.

And when the command to soften is given, the purpose is not retreat.

Softening is not withdrawal from difficulty. It is the opposite.

The intention behind softening is to restore flow so that you can press gently against whatever resistance is present and move through it. The contraction becomes the doorway. Instead of being obstructed by the mind’s reaction, you use the contraction as the point where movement resumes.

In this way difficulty stops functioning as a barrier and begins functioning as guidance.

The mind learns that contraction is not the end of movement. It is the place where the system adjusts itself. Each time you recognise the signal and soften into it, the reflex strengthens a little more.

Over time the practice becomes less deliberate and more natural. The moment of tightening is met almost immediately by the impulse to soften. And when that happens, flow does not need to be forced.

It returns on its own.
